Lesson Content

Lesson 1 — Science of Resuscitation

Objectives

Before playing the video, tell students what they should walk away with: why high-quality, minimally-interrupted CPR is the single biggest driver of survival, how the Chain of Survival links prehospital and in-hospital care, and how feedback devices and CPR coaching close the gap between what we think we're doing and what we're actually doing.

Sequence

  • State the learning objectives
  • Play the Science of Resuscitation video
  • Facilitated discussion: high-quality BLS, real-time feedback devices, CPR Coach role
  • Open floor for questions
  • Instructor summarizes key points before transitioning to hands-on practice

Lesson 2 — Compression / AED Practice & 2-Rescuer CPR

Session 1: Compression / AED Station Setup

  • Assign students to manikins, no more than 3 per manikin
  • Each student performs 1 minute of high-quality compressions while instructor observes rate, depth, and recoil, and while the student adjusts to real-time feedback-device and CPR Coach input
  • Instructor gives feedback to both the compressor and the CPR Coach after each rotation
Compression metrics: depth ≥2 in (5 cm) adult  •  rate 100–120/min  •  full chest recoil between compressions  •  hand placement centered on the lower half of the sternum

2-Rescuer CPR Rotation

Assign each student a number. Rotate through all three roles so every student is evaluated in each position; only one student is being formally evaluated at a time. Allow one retest per student if needed.

Round 1
S1 Compressor/Assessor · S2 AED · S3 CPR Coach
Round 2
S2 Compressor/Assessor · S3 AED · S1 CPR Coach
Round 3
S3 Compressor/Assessor · S1 AED · S2 CPR Coach

Lesson 3 — Airway Management

Framing before hands-on practice

Use a visible timer during practice so students ventilate at the correct rate and volume, and coach against ventilating too forcefully or too frequently. Explicitly connect over-ventilation to its physiologic cost before students touch a BVM:

Over-ventilation increases intrathoracic pressure, which impedes venous return and lowers cardiac output, cerebral blood flow, and coronary perfusion pressure — and it raises the risk of regurgitation and aspiration.

Sequence

  • Play the Airway Management video
  • Practice OPA/NPA insertion
  • Discuss oxygen delivery and suction setup
  • Practice 1-rescuer and 2-rescuer bag-mask ventilation (rotate ventilator/mask-seal roles between students)
  • Play the Advanced Airway video
  • Advanced airway (intubation) practice, scaled to available airway trainers/scope type

Lesson 4 — Monitor/Defibrillator & Equipment Tech Review

Hands-on walkthrough at the actual unit/training monitor before cases begin — students should never be seeing these controls for the first time during a scenario.

Monitor/defib functions

  • Power & mode selection
  • Transcutaneous pacing
  • Synchronized cardioversion
  • Non-invasive blood pressure
  • EtCO₂
  • Pulse oximetry
  • Defib pad connections
  • ECG lead placement — 3-lead, 4-lead, 5-lead

Also review

  • Crash cart / jump kit supply locations
  • Audiovisual feedback device setup for both practice and testing stations

Lesson 5 — Preventing Arrest: Bradycardia

  • Play the Bradycardia Algorithm video, answer questions
  • Discuss bradycardia management, including setup for transcutaneous pacing
  • Work through 3 bradycardia cases as a group
Keep the focus on treatment of stable vs. unstable bradycardia rather than fine differentiation between heart block types. Reinforce: students must call for rapid response/MET early when a patient shows signs of instability. Cover clinical deterioration signs, the definition of unstable signs/symptoms, and a brief review of first-degree, second-degree Type I, and second-degree Type II blocks — enough for pattern recognition, not a deep-dive.

Team Scenario

Every student runs one bradycardia scenario as Team Leader while teammates act in assigned roles and follow direction. Rotate roles below across the group; all students take part in an open-ended debrief after each scenario, focused on building a shared mental model of what happened — not on assigning blame.

Team Leader
Airway
IV/IO & Medications
Monitor/Defib
Compressor (as needed)
Timer / Recorder

Lesson 6 — Preventing Arrest: Tachycardia (Stable & Unstable)

State the video's learning objectives, then play the Tachycardia Algorithm video. Run the same case-and-debrief structure as Lesson 5, with treatment focused on synchronized cardioversion for the unstable patient.

Rhythm review

Cover stable vs. unstable presentation, then survey: sinus tachycardia, narrow-complex tachycardia, atrial fibrillation, atrial flutter, junctional rhythms, monomorphic V-tach, polymorphic V-tach with a pulse, torsades de pointes, and wide-complex tachycardia. Tie treatment decisions to local protocol where relevant.

Lesson 7 — High-Performance Teams

Frame this as in-hospital, out-of-hospital, or both, depending on your student mix.

  • Ask students what team behaviors they observed during today's scenarios — good and bad
  • Discuss timing and measurement in relation to survival (compression fraction, time to defibrillation, etc.)
  • Review the H's and T's of reversible causes
  • Discuss closed-loop communication, clear roles, and knowing your role under stress

Lesson Content

Lesson 1 — Adult CPR & AED

Sequence

  • Play the Adult CPR & AED video, pausing at built-in practice points
  • 1-rescuer adult CPR: compressions, ventilations, AED use
  • 2-rescuer adult CPR: rotate compressor / ventilator roles every 2 minutes or 5 cycles
Adult compression metrics: depth ≥2 in (5 cm)  •  rate 100–120/min  •  full recoil  •  minimize interruptions  •  30:2 compression-to-ventilation ratio (1-rescuer)
Compressor
Ventilator / AED Operator
Switch roles ~every 2 min

Lesson 2 — Child & Infant CPR

Cover the depth, ratio, and technique differences from adult CPR before students practice on pediatric manikins.

Child: depth ~2 in (at least 1/3 AP chest diameter)  •  rate 100–120/min  •  30:2 (1-rescuer) or 15:2 (2-rescuer).
Infant: depth ~1.5 in (at least 1/3 AP chest diameter)  •  2-finger technique (1-rescuer) or 2-thumb encircling technique (2-rescuer)  •  30:2 (1-rescuer) or 15:2 (2-rescuer).
  • Demonstrate the 2-thumb encircling technique for 2-rescuer infant CPR
  • Practice pediatric AED pad placement, including a pediatric attenuator when available

Lesson 3 — Choking Relief

  • Adult & child: abdominal thrusts while conscious; transition to CPR with visual airway checks if the victim becomes unresponsive
  • Infant: alternating back blows and chest thrusts while conscious; transition to CPR with visual airway checks if unresponsive

Lesson 4 — BVM Ventilation & Team Dynamics

  • Proper mask seal and barrier device use
  • Avoid excessive ventilation — watch rate and volume
  • Multi-rescuer team roles, closed-loop communication, clear role assignment

Lesson Content

Lesson 1 — Systematic Approach

  • Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT): Appearance, Work of Breathing, Circulation to Skin — assessed from across the room before touching the patient
  • Evaluate–Identify–Intervene cycle repeats throughout the encounter
  • Primary assessment: Airway, Breathing, Circulation, Disability, Exposure (ABCDE)

Lesson 2 — Pediatric High-Quality CPR

Child: depth ~2 in (≥1/3 AP chest diameter)  •  rate 100–120/min  •  15:2 (2-rescuer).
Infant: depth ~1.5 in (≥1/3 AP chest diameter)  •  2-thumb encircling technique for 2-rescuer  •  15:2 (2-rescuer).
  • Practice 1- and 2-rescuer pediatric CPR with feedback devices
  • Pediatric AED pad placement, including attenuator use where indicated
Compressor
Airway / Ventilations
AED / Monitor

Lesson 3 — Respiratory Emergencies

Distinguish respiratory distress from respiratory failure, then work through the four categories of respiratory problem so students can match the presentation to the right first intervention.

  • Upper airway obstruction
  • Lower airway obstruction
  • Lung tissue disease
  • Disordered control of breathing

Lesson 4 — Pediatric Airway Management

  • Correct OPA/NPA sizing for pediatric patients
  • Two-person BVM technique — one maintaining seal and head position, one squeezing the bag
  • Advanced airway considerations; confirm current cuffed vs. uncuffed ETT sizing guidance against the current PALS Provider Manual

Lesson 5 — Monitor/Defib & Vascular Access Tech Review

  • Pediatric pad sizing and attenuator use
  • ECG lead placement, EtCO₂, pulse oximetry, NIBP
  • IV/IO access sites and equipment for pediatric patients
  • Crash cart / code cart pediatric supply locations, including a length-based resuscitation tape if used

Lesson 6 — Shock Recognition & Management

  • Compensated vs. decompensated shock
  • Four types: hypovolemic, distributive, cardiogenic, obstructive
  • Reassess after every intervention — confirm current fluid/medication dosing against the PALS algorithm cards rather than memory

Lesson 7 — Bradycardia

  • Play the Bradycardia Algorithm video, answer questions
  • Distinguish bradycardia with vs. without cardiopulmonary compromise
  • Work through cases as a group before the team scenario

Team Scenario

Every student runs one scenario as Team Leader while teammates fill the remaining roles; rotate through the group and debrief openly after each run.

Lesson 8 — Tachycardia

  • Play the Tachycardia Algorithm video
  • Narrow vs. wide complex, stable vs. unstable
  • Treatment pathway: vagal maneuvers, medication therapy, synchronized cardioversion — confirm current weight-based dosing and energy settings against the PALS algorithm cards

Lesson 9 — Cardiac Arrest Algorithm & High-Performance Teams

  • Walk through the PALS Cardiac Arrest Algorithm
  • Review the H's and T's of reversible causes
  • Ask what team behaviors students observed today; discuss closed-loop communication and knowing your role

Two cards, one class. This is the standard ACLS agenda with a BLS Provider skills test and BLS exam added so students leave with both an ACLS and a BLS eCard. Per AHA rules, a BLS Instructor must be present to run the infant CPR skills test and the BLS exam, and the BLS card must be issued from the same Training Center as the ACLS card. Plan the BLS portion in advance and register students for it — it isn't something to tack on mid-class. Confirm the current official combined agenda and both skills checklists before use.

Adult + pediatric in one sitting — plan for a long day (or two). ACLS and PALS are each full advanced courses with their own manuals, algorithms, exams, and skills tests. Running them together makes for a very long single day; many Training Centers split this across two days. The agendas below assume all precourse work (Self-Assessment ≥70% + video prework) is complete for both programs before class, and that shared foundations (high-quality CPR, airway, team dynamics) are taught once and applied to both adult and pediatric patients. Students receive separate ACLS and PALS eCards. Confirm both official agendas, skills checklists, and current pediatric dosing against the respective Instructor Manuals before use.
